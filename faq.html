Frequently Asked Questions

A Personalized Recovery Plan is a customized plan designed to help individuals overcome addiction by providing tailored advice and strategies based on their unique recovery stage, goals, and type of addiction.

Addiction is a chronic disease characterized by the compulsive use of a substance or engagement in a behavior, despite harmful consequences. It alters the brain's chemistry and can lead to dependency, making it difficult for individuals to stop without help. It can affect substances like alcohol, drugs, or behaviors like gambling.

Signs of addiction can include:
  • Increased tolerance (needing more of the substance or behavior to achieve the same effect)
  • Withdrawal symptoms when not engaging in the behavior or using the substance
  • Loss of control over usage
  • Continued use despite negative consequences
  • Preoccupation with the substance or behavior

Yes, addiction can be treated. Treatment typically involves a combination of therapies, support groups, and sometimes medication, depending on the type of addiction. Some common treatments include cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), medication-assisted treatment (MAT), and inpatient or outpatient rehabilitation programs.

Addiction can result from a combination of genetic, environmental, and psychological factors. These may include:
  • Genetic predisposition to addiction
  • Chronic stress or trauma
  • Peer pressure or a social environment that encourages substance use
  • Mental health conditions such as depression or anxiety
  • The availability of addictive substances or behaviors

While often used interchangeably, addiction and dependence are slightly different. Dependence occurs when the body adapts to a substance, requiring more to achieve the same effect (tolerance), and experiences withdrawal symptoms when the substance is not available. Addiction, however, is characterized by compulsive behavior despite negative consequences. Someone may be dependent on a substance but not necessarily addicted.

While some people may be able to reduce or control their use on their own, most individuals benefit from professional treatment and support. Addiction often requires comprehensive care, which may include therapy, counseling, support groups, and sometimes medical intervention. Trying to recover without assistance can be difficult and increases the risk of relapse.
